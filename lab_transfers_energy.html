Why Each Medium Behaves Differently
All three media show exponential amplitude decay with distance — energy loss is proportional to the energy remaining at each point. What differs is the rate and mechanism. Solid damping via internal friction tends to be moderate; liquid (hydraulic) damping via viscous shear is typically steeper at higher frequencies; gas (aerodynamic) shows the weakest classical bulk damping, meaning sound travels the farthest in air before significant energy loss from these mechanisms.
Solid: Internal friction converts mechanical vibration to heat. Lower frequencies travel further — relevant to bone conduction in the skull.
Liquid: Viscous shear between fluid layers dissipates energy. Thicker fluid = steeper decay. Relevant in otitis media — fluid in the middle ear significantly attenuates the signal.
Gas: Weakest classical bulk damping among the three. The ear canal and middle ear air space introduce relatively little energy loss compared to a fluid-filled space.
Part 3
Basilar Membrane Tonotopy
The basilar membrane is stiff and narrow at the base (high frequencies) and wide and flexible at the apex (low frequencies). Different input frequencies cause maximum displacement at different locations — this is called tonotopy. Adjust the frequency slider to see where along the membrane that frequency resonates.

Frequency Region
The basilar membrane is ~35 mm long and performs all frequency analysis before any neural processing occurs. It is the ear's built-in spectrum analyzer.
Speech energy is concentrated between roughly 500–4,000 Hz — this occupies the middle third of the basilar membrane.
Noise-induced hearing loss typically damages the 4,000 Hz region first — it sits near the base where the membrane is stiffest and receives maximum mechanical stress.
