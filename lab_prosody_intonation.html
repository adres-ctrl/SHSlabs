Key Concept
What actually changes acoustically?
When stress is added to a syllable, the vowel nucleus carries most of the change. Vowels are continuous and sonorant — they have a wide range for F₀ variation, a large dynamic range for amplitude, and can be lengthened substantially without losing identity. Consonants are also stressed, but their acoustic changes are much smaller because their manner of production limits how much each can vary.
F₀ is controlled primarily by the cricothyroid muscle — tensing and elongating the vocal folds raises pitch.
Amplitude is controlled by subglottal air pressure — increased respiratory drive pushes the folds apart further, releasing more air per cycle.
Duration is controlled by respiratory pacing — slowing the airflow and extending the gesture lengthens the sound.
