Total
The sum of all data points. Useful for knowing how much of something occurred across an entire sample — e.g., total words produced in a session.
Max
The single highest value recorded. Tells you the best performance the patient achieved under any condition in that sample.
Min
The single lowest value. Helps identify the floor — the hardest conditions or worst performance observed.
Median
The middle value when sorted. Resistant to outliers — useful when a few extreme trials might skew the average.
Mean
The arithmetic average. Sensitive to outliers. Commonly reported in therapy notes and research as the central estimate of performance.
Part 2
Temporal Measures
Time-based data has three distinct forms — and they are not interchangeable. Select a mode below to explore each one with a live tool. Understanding which type you are collecting changes everything about how you calculate and report a rate.
Sample Time — the observation window
The total stretch of time from which data is collected. You define the window first, then count events within it. Events outside the window do not count.
e.g., "collect data during a 30-second spontaneous speech sample"
Window:
0:30.0
The sample window is the denominator of every rate you calculate. A 30-second and a 60-second window with the same number of events produce very different rates — which is exactly why you must define the window before counting anything. Head to Part 3 to count events and build your rate.
Time Points — anchoring your window
Time points are the start and end markers that define where your sample sits within a longer recording. The sample does not have to begin at zero. You choose the window by marking its boundaries.
e.g., "I started collecting at 0:18 and stopped at 1:48 — that 90-second stretch is my sample"
Run the timer as if playing a recording. Mark your start and end points anywhere within it to define your data window.

Duration — how long a single event lasts
Duration measures the length of one specific behavior — a sustained vowel, a pause, a block, an utterance. Each press captures one event. Multiple durations can then be analyzed as a dataset using count statistics from Part 1.
e.g., "Patient sustained /a/ for 6.2s, 8.4s, and 7.1s — mean duration = 7.2s"

Goal Writing Guide

Completeness
Self-Check
The action is specific — it describes a behavior, not a feeling or state.
The criteria uses a number or quantity — not a vague word like "better" or "improved."
The criteria is directly tied to the action — the thing being measured actually reflects the skill being practiced.
The condition specifies the context — structured task, conversation, reading, etc.
The condition specifies the cueing level — independent, minimal, moderate, or maximal cues.
A clinician who has never met this patient could understand and track this goal.
There is a clear endpoint — a point at which the patient passes this goal.
This goal can show a client's progression in a short amount of time — small improvements should be visible in the data, not hidden until the goal is fully met.
Reflective Questions

Could this goal go on forever? If a patient who never improves can still technically continue working on this goal without a clear pass/fail, the criteria needs work.

Does the measure match the outcome? If you are tracking phonation amplitude in dB but the goal is about sentence length, these are not connected. Ask: would improving this number actually reflect the skill you are treating?

Is the accuracy percentage meaningful? "80% accuracy" requires you to define what counts as one opportunity. Without that, 80% of what?

Would a different clinician write this the same way? If the goal requires insider knowledge of your methods or terminology, it is not objective enough.
